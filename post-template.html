The morning I decided to stop pretending I had it together.

February 18, 2026 6 min read

There's a version of yourself you perform for the world — polished, certain, moving forward with intention. Then there's the Tuesday morning version. I've been thinking about which one actually builds anything worth keeping.

It started with a meeting I wasn't prepared for. Not catastrophically unprepared — just the kind of low-grade unreadiness that you can usually paper over with confidence and a few smart questions. Except this time, I couldn't. And instead of the usual adrenaline that kicks in when you're winging it professionally, I felt something quieter: relief.

The performance was exhausting. And I hadn't realized how much I'd been performing until the moment I stopped.

I've spent a lot of years getting good at looking like I have it together. It's not dishonest, exactly — it's more like a habit of presentation that develops so gradually you stop noticing it's happening. You learn which parts of yourself to lead with. Which doubts to keep private. Which questions signal competence versus which ones signal gaps.

The cost of performing certainty

What nobody tells you about projecting confidence is how much energy it takes. Not the confidence itself — that can be genuine. But the relentless management of how you're being perceived: the editing, the calibrating, the reading of rooms. It sits underneath everything like background noise you eventually stop hearing.

That morning, the noise stopped. And the silence was interesting.

I told my team I hadn't looked at the numbers as closely as I should have. I asked the question I normally would have Googled in secret beforehand. I said "I don't know yet" to something I would usually have faked my way through with a confident-sounding non-answer.

Nothing fell apart. A few things actually got better — the conversation got more honest, and two people said things they'd clearly been holding back. We made a better decision than we would have if I'd arrived polished and certain and performing.

I'm not making an argument for being unprepared. I'm making an argument for the particular kind of credibility that comes from letting people see you think, rather than only letting them see your conclusions.

There's a version of leadership — and of personhood, really — that's about always having the answer. And there's another version that's about being trustworthy enough that people believe you when you say you're working on it. I'm more interested in the second one. I think it's harder. I think it's worth it.

We proceed, even when we're figuring it out as we go. Especially then.
